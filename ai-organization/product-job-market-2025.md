# State of the Product Job Market — 2025

**Source:** Lenny Rachitsky with [TrueUp](https://trueup.io/) data, [Lenny's Newsletter](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/state-of-the-product-job-market-in-2025) (2025-05-13)

Mid-2025 snapshot of PM, engineering, and design hiring. Precedes the 2026 snapshot ([[ai-organization/product-job-market-2026|2026 version]]) and updates the 2024 two-part series.

## Headline trends

1. **Open PM + engineering roles trending up.** 6,000+ open PM roles globally — 53.6% above the 2023 bottom, 11% up YTD. Engineering growth looks similar (10× scale). Highest counts since late 2022.
2. **AI roles exploding.** 688 open AI PM roles alone. All "AI" roles (AI-cos + AI-adjacent cos + AI-specific roles at non-AI cos) growing even faster.
3. **Layoffs slowing.** On track for the lowest annual total in four years — but still a live risk at top companies.
4. **Capital investment at all-time highs.** +29% YoY, +227% over five years (GPUs, data centers). Unclear yet whether this converts to more *jobs* or just more hardware.
5. **Bay Area still dominant.** ~20% of open PM roles, 17%+ of engineering, and nearly **1/3 of all AI-related roles globally**.
6. **Remote declining to a new baseline.** Peak ~35% of PM roles remote-friendly (late 2022); now ~23%. Likely settling near ~20%. Identical pattern for engineering and design.
7. **Lots of open roles worldwide.** Berlin and Austin entered top-10 PM cities; Boston and LA fell out.

## Emerging trends to watch

- **Rise of the Product Engineer** — 415+ open roles (v0, Amplitude, n8n). Hybrid PM/SWE title growing.
- **AI copilot impact on PM interviewing** — anecdotal so far; likely to reshape interview formats.

## Key Takeaways

- The "recession / AI ate the jobs" narrative doesn't match the data — open PM and eng roles are near a multi-year high.
- **AI PM is the hottest product role** — 688 explicitly-AI PM roles, global AI roles compounding faster.
- **Bay Area gravity is re-concentrating** especially for AI work (~33% of AI roles).
- **Remote work is structurally lower** than the 2022 peak and unlikely to recover.
- Layoffs decelerating; capital investment accelerating — but the conversion from capex → hiring is still TBD.
