# Product Strategy and the Product Life Cycle

**Source:** Roman Pichler — [The Product Strategy and the Product Life Cycle](https://www.romanpichler.com/blog/the-product-strategy-and-the-product-life-cycle/) (2024)

---

## Summary

The product lifecycle model (Development → Introduction → Growth → Maturity → Decline) is a sense-making tool, not a forecast. It helps product people understand where a product sits in its life, how much strategy effort is required at each stage, and what strategic posture is appropriate.

---

## The Five Stages

| Stage | Focus | Strategy Effort | Mindset |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Development** | Get to launch; serve early adopters | Very high (discovery + experimentation) | Offensive, entrepreneurial |
| **Introduction** | Achieve product-market fit (PMF); bridge the chasm | High (pivot-ready) | Adaptive, ready to pivot |
| **Growth** | Sustain growth, differentiate from competitors | Moderate (maintain PMF, fend off copies) | Expansion, differentiation |
| **Maturity** | Extend the lifecycle OR manage as cash cow | Low-to-moderate (incremental) | Defensive, efficient |
| **Decline** | Accept end-of-life OR reposition as niche | Low (unless resurrection attempted) | Winding down or repositioning |

---

## Key Events in the Lifecycle

- **Launch** — MVP becomes available to the early market
- **Product-Market Fit (PMF)** — product is ready for the mainstream; growth begins
- **Life Cycle Extension** — new strategy to renew growth (new segment, new need, new model)
- **End of Life** — product is discontinued

---

## Strategic Implications Per Stage

**Development:** Strategy targets innovators and early adopters — a narrow segment willing to tolerate early imperfections. Validate the strategy iteratively before committing to build.

**Introduction:** If response is poor, pivot or kill. If positive, resist optimising for the early market — shift the strategy toward mainstream users.

**Growth:** Two main challenges: (1) growing audience diversity requires unbundling or product variants; (2) competitor copying erodes differentiation. Use Strategy Canvas to track and restore differentiation.

**Maturity:** Two options — extend lifecycle (strategy update required, new risks to validate) or manage as cash cow (stable strategy, efficiency focus). Either way, continuous strategizing continues.

**Decline:** Accept end-of-life or attempt niche repositioning. A niche repositioning requires a full strategy rework.

---

## The Chasm

Between Introduction and Growth lies a gap: early adopters differ significantly from mainstream buyers. Bridging it typically requires:
- Adjusting the strategy (new target group, updated needs)
- Feature additions or architecture changes
- New business model elements

Most products that fail do so in the introduction stage — either by not reaching PMF or by failing to bridge the chasm.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Lifecycle stage determines strategy effort** — don't apply Development-level strategy intensity to a mature product, and don't under-invest in a product still searching for PMF
- **The model is descriptive, not predictive** — use KPIs to track where you actually are
- **Strategy is never static** — even mature products require continuous monitoring; decline is not inevitable
- **Multiple lifecycles are possible** — a product can be rejuvenated (extended) multiple times (iPhone is the canonical example)
- Kill decisions are strategic: discontinuing a product frees resources for products that still create value
