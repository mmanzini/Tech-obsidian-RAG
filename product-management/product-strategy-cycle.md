# The Product Strategy Cycle

**Source:** Roman Pichler — [The Product Strategy Cycle](https://www.romanpichler.com/blog/the-product-strategy-cycle/) (2020)

---

## Summary

An iterative process that systematically links strategy and execution. Strategy and execution are not separate, sequential phases — they must be continuously connected. Insights from delivery feed back into strategy adaptation; the strategy guides which product goals to pursue.

---

## The Cycle

```
Product Strategy
       ↓
Product Roadmap (product goals, 12-month outcomes)
       ↓
Product Backlog (features derived from the next goal)
       ↓
Product (developed iteratively)
       ↓
KPIs (measure if the goal was met + overall health)
       ↓
Review & Adapt → back to Product Strategy
```

Each step flows from the previous. When KPIs indicate a problem, revise the roadmap or strategy immediately — don't wait for the cycle to complete.

---

## Key Transitions

**Strategy → Roadmap:**
Translate strategic intent (target group, needs, business goals) into specific time-bounded outcomes (product goals). A product goal describes what benefit or outcome the product should achieve, not what features to build.

**Roadmap → Backlog:**
Pick the next product goal from the roadmap. Determine the features required to meet it. Only add enough items to start the next sprint — avoid over-specification upfront.

**Product → KPIs:**
Measure two things: (1) Was the product goal met? (2) Is the overall product healthy? Sample KPIs: engagement, retention, product quality, revenue, team motivation.

**KPIs → Strategy:**
If KPIs show the strategy is not working, adapt — possibly pivot. The cycle is designed for continuous strategy adaptation, not one-time strategy creation.

---

## The Collaboration Dimension

The cycle only works if stakeholders and development team representatives are involved in:
- Creating and validating the strategy
- Developing and reviewing the roadmap
- Tracking KPIs and development progress
- Updating the plans as insights emerge

Collaborative decision-making (consent, workshops, sprint reviews) is how buy-in is created. A strategy without buy-in cannot be executed.

---

## What Can Trigger Cycle Breaks

- KPIs reveal the strategy is wrong → adapt or pivot
- Backlog changes expose a flawed roadmap assumption → update roadmap
- Competitive threat or technology shift requires a larger strategy update → run Strategy Discovery

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Strategy and execution are not sequential** — they are continuously connected through the cycle
- **Product goals** (not features) are the unit of the roadmap — outcomes, not outputs
- **KPIs serve two functions**: verify goal achievement AND signal strategy health
- Involve stakeholders and developers throughout — a strategy they didn't help shape won't get executed
- **Continuous strategizing** (half a day per week, per Pichler) keeps you responsive without a full strategy restart
