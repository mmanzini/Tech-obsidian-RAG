# Essential Reading for Product Builders — Part 1

**Source:** Lenny Rachitsky, [Lenny's Newsletter](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/essential-reading-for-product-builderspart) (2025-07-15)

Seven timeless essays Lenny finds himself quoting repeatedly. The framing, via Marc Andreessen: *"barbell your inputs — read things that are either up-to-the-minute or timeless; avoid anything from yesterday through 10 years ago."*

## The seven essays

### 1. [Who's Got the Monkey](https://www.med.unc.edu/uncaims/wp-content/uploads/sites/764/2014/03/Oncken-_-Wass-Who_s-Got-the-Monkey.pdf) — Oncken & Wass (HBR)
The "monkey" = next-move responsibility. Every time a manager says *"let me think about it"*, they've accepted a monkey that belonged to their report. Compounds into subordinate-imposed time. Hand the monkey back; the report supervises you otherwise.

### 2. [On Agency](https://www.henrikkarlsson.xyz/p/agency) — Henrik Karlsson
Agency = **autonomy** (formulating your own goals) × **efficacy** (willingness and ability to pursue them). Not the type-A hard-edge stereotype — often "almost gentle... like the force of a river being pulled to the sea." The opposite is playing social games or passivity toward solvable problems.

### 3. [Building Good Taste](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=X2wLP0izeJE) — Ira Glass (video)
The "taste gap": everyone who does creative work starts with good taste but bad output. Most quit during the gap. The only cure is **volume** — ship a lot on deadlines. The gap closes when output catches up to taste.

### 4. [Strategies for Learning](https://andymasley.substack.com/p/strategies-for-learning) — Andy Masley
"Pretending to learn feels good; actual learning feels bad." Many read books for the identity of being a reader, retain nothing. Learning is psychologically difficult because it's low-status in the moment. Don't confuse the performance with the practice.

### 5. [If Your Product Is Great, It Doesn't Need To Be Good](https://paulbuchheit.blogspot.com/2010/02/if-your-product-is-great-it-doesnt-need.html) — Paul Buchheit
Pick **three** key attributes/features, get them very right, forget the rest. Original iPod: pocketable, hours of music, Mac-syncable. Original Gmail: fast, stored everything, conversation+search UI. Address book was 2 engineer-days because it didn't matter. "If your product needs everything to be good, it's probably not innovative."

### 6. [The Only Thing That Matters](https://fictivekin.github.io/pmarchive-jekyll/guide_to_startups_part4) — Marc Andreessen
Team vs. product vs. market — most answer *team*. Andreessen's claim: **market** wins. A great market *pulls* product out of a startup. The first viable product will fill it; the market doesn't care how elegant the team was. Corollary: avoid great teams and great products chasing non-existent markets.

### 7. [The Cook and the Chef — Musk's Secret Sauce](https://waitbutwhy.com/2015/11/the-cook-and-the-chef-musks-secret-sauce.html) — Tim Urban
First-principles reasoning as the differentiator. A cook follows recipes (imitating conventional wisdom); a chef reasons from physics/truth and cooks new dishes. Musk reasons at the chef level on problems everyone else inherits as recipes.

## Key Takeaways

- **Keep monkeys off your back.** Don't accept next-move responsibility for other people's problems.
- **Agency = autonomy + efficacy.** Both halves are necessary; hard-edged is not the only mode.
- **Close the taste gap with volume**, not cleverness. Deadlines > inspiration.
- **Beware performative learning** — retain, don't decorate.
- **Three features, done right.** "If it's great, it doesn't have to be good."
- **Market pulls product.** Team matters only insofar as it can ship something viable into a pulling market.
- **Reason from first principles**, not recipes. Cooks vs. chefs.
