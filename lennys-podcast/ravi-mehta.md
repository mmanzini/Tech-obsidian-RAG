# Ravi Mehta — The Product Strategy Stack

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast, Ravi Mehta (Outpace, ex-Tinder CPO, Facebook, TripAdvisor)

Ravi Mehta's operating system for product leaders: a five-layer strategy stack, an NCT-based approach to risk, and the heretical claim that goals should come *after* the roadmap, not before. His Tinder "Platinum tier" story is a case study in whale-driven product design.

## The Product Strategy Stack (five layers)
1. **Mission** — why the company exists
2. **Strategy** — how the company will win in the market
3. **Product strategy** — how the product delivers on the strategy
4. **Roadmap** — what you're going to build and in what order
5. **Goals** — what numerical outcomes the work is expected to produce

- Contrarian ordering: build the *roadmap before the goals*. Most orgs invert this (set OKRs, then back into work), which produces goals disconnected from capacity and a feature list disconnected from outcomes.

## Latency vs. velocity
- Velocity = features shipped per unit time.
- Latency = time from insight → shipped impact on a user.
- Startups over-optimize velocity and under-optimize latency. The winning teams reduce latency; everyone else celebrates throughput.

## Conviction > experimentation (at startup scale)
- A/B testing requires volume startups don't have.
- Instead: form strong hypotheses, ship with conviction, learn qualitatively. Experimentation is a late-stage scaling tool.

## NCTs over OKRs: the frontier of understanding
- **NCTs = Narrative, Commitments, Tasks.** A unit of strategy that includes the *story* of why.
- Surface the "frontier of understanding" — name the four risk types explicitly:
  - **Understanding risk** — do we know the problem?
  - **Dependency risk** — do we control the inputs?
  - **Execution risk** — can we build it?
  - **Strategic risk** — will it matter if we do?
- Plans fail when the team can't articulate which risk class is dominant.

## The 12 PM competencies
- Four competency areas, three sub-skills each:
  - **Execution** — project management, attention to detail, decision-making
  - **Customer insight** — qualitative research, quantitative analysis, product sense
  - **Strategy** — vision, market understanding, prioritization
  - **Leadership** — communication, influence, team building
- Use the grid for hiring rubrics, career ladders, and self-assessment, not for performance reviews.

## Selective micromanagement
- 2×2 matrix: alignment (low↔high) × confidence (low↔high).
- High alignment + high confidence → full autonomy
- High alignment + low confidence → coaching
- Low alignment + high confidence → directive; re-align
- Low alignment + low confidence → replace the person or the scope
- Micromanagement isn't bad — *indiscriminate* micromanagement is. Apply it where confidence is low, not universally.

## Tinder whales → Platinum tier + super-like-with-note
- Data discovery at Tinder: a tiny cohort of power users drove an outsized share of revenue.
- Response: build a Platinum tier specifically for whales, including "super-like-with-note" — a monetizable feature whales *actually wanted* but the rest of the base didn't need.
- Principle: segment revenue, not just users, and design for the segment that pays.

## Wireframes inside strategy docs
- Ravi's strategy docs embed rough wireframes alongside narrative.
- Reason: concrete artifacts force real decisions. A pretty narrative with no wireframe is almost always dodging a hard call.

## Key Takeaways
- Use the Product Strategy Stack: mission → strategy → product strategy → roadmap → goals, in that order.
- Latency matters more than velocity at startup scale.
- Ship on conviction until you have the traffic to A/B test honestly.
- Replace OKRs with NCTs; name your dominant risk class out loud.
- The 12-competency grid is the right shape for hiring and growth, not reviews.
- Micromanage only at the low-confidence, low-alignment corners.
- Segment your revenue. Build for the whales if whales pay the bills.
- Put wireframes in the strategy doc.
