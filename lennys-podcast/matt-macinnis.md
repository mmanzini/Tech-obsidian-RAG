# Matt MacInnis — Deliberately Understaff, and Other Heresies

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast — Former COO Rippling, operator/advisor

Matt's thesis: the default operating assumption in Silicon Valley — hire ahead of need, run process-heavy orgs, trust VC advice — systematically suppresses the rare outcomes companies actually need. His counter-playbook is built from Rippling's hyper-output years.

## Deliberate Understaffing

- "Over-staffing is poison" — slack capacity kills urgency and invites process.
- Keep every team slightly under the workload — forces ruthless prioritization.
- Painful in the moment but produces the **extraordinary efforts** that generate 99th-percentile outcomes.
- Opposite of the "hire ahead of the curve" default; Matt considers it the single highest-leverage choice a COO makes.

## Alpha and Beta — Process Is a Tax on Upside

- Borrow from finance: **alpha** = above-market outcomes, **beta** = market-correlated baseline.
- Process reduces variance (lower beta) but also **suppresses alpha** — the extreme wins come from un-processed action.
- Growth-stage companies install process faster than they need it and lose their alpha cohort.
- Rule: only add process when a specific failure has happened twice.

## Product Quality List (PQL) — One Feature Flag Rule

- Every release carries a **PQL** — a written list of known quality defects.
- Hard rule: **one and only one feature flag at a time** across the whole product.
- Multiple feature flags create combinatorial states nobody can reason about or test.
- Forces sequencing discipline and makes the PQL readable by humans.

## SPOTAC — Hiring for Rare Compound Traits

- **S**mart, **P**assionate, **O**ptimistic, **T**enacious, **A**daptable, **C** (Kind).
- All six required, not a subset. Most candidates fail on tenacity or kindness.
- Optimism isn't naivety — it's the ability to keep moving when facts are bad.
- Hiring for SPOTAC is expensive up front and dramatically cheaper downstream.

## Receptors and Drugs — PMF Is Physics, Not Marketing

- Analogy: your product is a drug; the market has receptors that either bind it or don't.
- If receptors exist, the product takes off; if not, no amount of marketing creates them.
- You cannot "market through" missing PMF — you can only find another receptor (pivot the wedge).
- Implication: spend disproportionate time on wedge selection before scaling.

## Learn from Successes, Not Only Mistakes

- Standard advice: do post-mortems on failures.
- Matt's addition: **do the same forensic analysis on wins**.
- Success is often accidental and reproducible; failure is often unique and not.
- Codify the conditions that produced the win into the next bet.

## Narrative Violations and the "Try Until You Die" Trap

- VC "try until you die" propaganda is pay-to-play — it serves portfolio math, not founders.
- Shutting down or pivoting isn't failure; running a zombie is.
- Narrative violations (silent strategy changes) burn team trust faster than bad quarters.

## Key Takeaways

- Deliberately understaff — slack is the enemy of extraordinary outcomes.
- Process lowers beta and alpha; only add it after the same failure repeats.
- Enforce one feature flag at a time; maintain a visible PQL.
- Hire SPOTAC — all six traits, no subsets.
- PMF is physics (receptors bind or not) — don't try to market through its absence.
- Study your wins as carefully as your losses.
