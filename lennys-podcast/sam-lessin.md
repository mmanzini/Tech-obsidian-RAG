# Sam Lessin — Low Heart Rate in a Crowded Room

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast, Sam Lessin (Slow Ventures, ex-Facebook)

Sam Lessin's frame: etiquette is showing up in a room with a low heart rate. Abundance mindset beats scarcity, repeat names, clean up after yourself, and — contrarian take — don't invest in "AI companies."

## Etiquette = low heart rate
- Good manners aren't rules; they're the visible evidence that you're not panicking.
- A person with a low heart rate in a meeting is signalling abundance — they don't need this deal, this hire, this intro.
- Scarcity mindset is visible in micro-behaviors: over-selling, interrupting, clinging to Calendly control.

## Introductions and names
- Repeat someone's name inside the first minute; it telegraphs presence and locks the memory.
- Partner-introduction trick: when you introduce your partner, their name goes *first* — it flips the default hierarchy and says "this person matters more than the job."
- Leave them wanting more. End the meeting 5 minutes early, not 5 minutes late.

## Conversation as ping-pong, not inquisition
- Asking questions is good; asking a sequence of questions is an interrogation.
- Volley — ask, share a little, ask again. The ratio of ask-to-offer should feel like a rally.

## Calendly is a status signal
- If you're the lower-status party in a relationship, you do *not* send a Calendly link. You ask when works and you flex.
- Sending Calendly to someone senior to you reads as "my time equals your time" — which is both wrong and rude.

## Email as protocol
- The order of names in the CC/BCC field is a status and attention signal.
- Subject-line discipline > long email bodies.
- BCC sparingly — it's the political nuclear option.

## Meeting etiquette
- Camera on, always.
- "Close the closet" — tidy your background; it's free professionalism.
- Irish goodbye when the meeting is over. Protracted farewells signal low confidence.
- Clean up after yourself. Sam's LP story: a junior partner was judged not on pitch quality but on whether they bussed their own coffee cup.

## The AI-investing contrarian corner
- Sam's seed-stage take: AI-branded startups will mostly lose money.
- Too capital-intensive, too commoditized, too many me-too wrappers.
- OpenAI at seed would have been a "middling at best" investment on risk-adjusted terms.
- Better target: businesses that *use* AI as leverage but aren't trying to *be* AI companies.

## Terra Nova narrative
- Investable new-world stories come every ~20 years: the American West → Internet → Crypto.
- Each had a genuine frontier that rewarded settlers. AI doesn't feel like Terra Nova to him — it feels like electricity, i.e. infrastructure, not territory.
- Where's the new Terra Nova? Still open. Possibly biology, possibly space, possibly something we don't recognize yet.

## Letter Meme — built, not outsourced
- Sam built Letter Meme, an AI-cartoon newsletter aggregator, mostly himself.
- Point: if the AI-app moat is thin enough that a GP can build one on nights and weekends, the category is not a seed-stage investable class.

## Key Takeaways
- Etiquette is heart rate management; everything else follows.
- Abundance beats scarcity, and scarcity is always visible in micro-behaviors.
- Don't Calendly upward. Don't nibble goodbyes.
- Invest in businesses that *use* AI; don't invest in "AI companies."
- The real next frontier may not be AI — it may be whatever looks like AI's electricity moment 20 years from now.
- If you can build the app in a weekend, it isn't a company.
