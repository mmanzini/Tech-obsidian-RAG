# Jessica Fain — Executive calendars are strobe lights: how to influence without politics

Source: `sources/jessica-fain.md` (Lenny's Podcast, product leader, ex-Mapbox/Asana)

Fain's thesis separates **influence** (getting the org to the right answer) from **politics** (getting credit or territory). Her toolkit is specifically designed around the reality that executives see your problem for 30–60 seconds at a time — their calendars are strobe lights, not movies. The frameworks are pre-written moves for specific high-leverage moments.

## Influence vs. politics
- Influence: shift the org's understanding/decision toward what's right.
- Politics: shift the org's perception toward who gets credit.
- The two feel similar from inside but have opposite long-term outcomes — influence compounds, politics decays.
- Test: am I making the decision better, or making myself look better in this decision?

## Executive calendars are strobe lights
- An exec sees your topic for 30–60 seconds at a time, with gaps of days or weeks in between.
- Every interaction must independently re-establish context — you cannot assume they remember where you left off.
- Opening move: 30–60 seconds of context before the ask. Not more. Not less.

## "Not my fault but my problem" (Annie Pearl)
- The senior operator's move: claim problems that aren't your fault because they're now your problem.
- Avoids the junior failure mode of "that's the other team's issue" — which is correct attribution but terrible outcome.
- Expands your effective scope without formal org change.

## "That's so interesting. What led you to believe that?"
- The universal disagree-without-escalating phrase.
- Buys time, signals respect, and surfaces the reasoning underneath the position.
- Often the other person walks themselves back once they articulate the why.

## Pre-review — "Hey Stuart, what do you think?"
- Before the big meeting, walk the doc past key stakeholders 1:1.
- Goal: no surprises in the room. Decisions made in rooms with surprises are usually bad decisions.
- Pre-review also lets you incorporate feedback and build coalition before anyone is performing for a group.

## Stuart + 2 more options
- When presenting a recommendation, offer the recommended option plus two alternatives.
- Exec gets to exercise judgment (which they must, to feel ownership) while being guided.
- Two alternatives, not one — one feels like a straw man, three feels like real choice.

## Shrink the change
- From the Heath brothers' *Switch*: big asks get refused; small asks get accepted.
- Decompose the big change into the smallest version that still moves the org and lead with that.
- Momentum compounds — the next ask gets easier.

## Document hygiene
- Topics-for-discussion at the top of every doc — what you actually need from this meeting.
- Minto Pyramid structure: answer first, then supporting arguments, then data.
- Most exec-time waste is burying the ask.

## Reading the room above you
- Don't ask your manager "what's top of mind?" — too open, they'll give you last week's answer.
- Ask: "what is the board pushing the CEO on right now?" — gets you two levels up, shows you see the system.
- Take the breadcrumb thread — follow the interesting thing they say, don't return to your agenda.

## Key Takeaways
- Influence compounds; politics decays. Test: making the decision better, or making yourself look better?
- Exec calendars are strobe lights — 30–60 seconds of context every time, no assumption of continuity.
- "Not my fault but my problem" is the senior-operator claim on scope.
- "That's so interesting. What led you to believe that?" is the universal de-escalating disagreement move.
- Pre-review every important doc 1:1 before the group meeting — no surprises in the room.
- Present recommended option + 2 alternatives; shrink the change to the smallest version that moves the org.
- Ask "what is the board pushing the CEO on?" not "what's top of mind?"
