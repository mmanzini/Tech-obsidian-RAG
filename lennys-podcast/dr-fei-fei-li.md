# Dr. Fei-Fei Li — Spatial intelligence and the humanist AI: past the cave, into the world

Source: `Raw/dr-fei-fei-li.md` (Lenny's Podcast, ImageNet creator, World Labs founder)

Fei-Fei Li's thesis: today's LLMs are Plato's cave — linguistic shadows of a 3D world they've never seen. The next frontier is **spatial intelligence** — world models that understand geometry, physics, and embodiment. Her frameworks are anchored in the ImageNet story (the three-ingredient recipe that birthed modern AI), intellectual fearlessness, and "humanist AI — nothing artificial about it."

## The three ingredients of modern AI (2012)
- ImageNet (2009): 15M images, 22K concepts — the dataset that made learning-from-scale possible.
- Neural nets: the algorithmic ingredient (convolutional architectures, scaled deep).
- GPUs: the compute ingredient that made the first two tractable.
- AlexNet (2012) combined all three; this was the Big Bang, not a gradual climb.
- Lesson: breakthrough requires all three ingredients simultaneously — missing one and nothing happens.

## "AI was a dirty word" (2016)
- As late as 2016, saying "AI" in academic/industry contexts was a status marker for unserious.
- The field had burned credibility in prior winters; serious people said "machine learning" instead.
- Context for how fast the Overton window moved — and a reminder that the current consensus can invert in 5 years.

## Plato's cave for vision — why language isn't enough
- LLMs are trained on text about the world, which is a projection (shadows on a wall) of the 3D physical reality.
- Language models can describe the cave wall brilliantly but don't know what casts the shadows.
- Spatial intelligence = learning the geometry, physics, and dynamics of the world itself — not just text about it.

## Spatial intelligence / world models (World Labs, Marble)
- Next layer of the stack: models that generate and reason about 3D space, not 2D images or tokens.
- Marble: early product instantiation — AI-generated explorable worlds.
- Applications cascade: robotics, design, simulation, embodied agents — all require a world model to be competent.

## The bitter lesson + why robotics needs more
- Sutton's bitter lesson: scale + general methods beats handcrafted cleverness in the long run.
- Fei-Fei's caveat: robotics needs the bitter lesson *plus* much more — the physical world has no internet-scale dataset, so the data flywheel has to be invented.
- Can't just wait for GPT-for-robots — the ingredient mix is different.

## Humanist AI — "nothing artificial about AI"
- "AI" as a label misleads: these systems are built by humans, shaped by human data, deployed on humans.
- Ethics, governance, and impact aren't bolted on — they're the substrate.
- Humanist AI = explicit framing that keeps human outcomes as the optimization target, not a downstream metric.

## Intellectual fearlessness as career advice
- Pick problems that scare you intellectually — the fear is a signal you're near frontier.
- Work from first principles; don't defer to the consensus of the field, especially when the field just shifted.
- ImageNet was mocked early (dataset work was low-prestige); the fearlessness was the move.

## Key Takeaways
- Modern AI required three ingredients simultaneously — data (ImageNet), neural nets, GPUs. AlexNet 2012 was the ignition.
- As recently as 2016, "AI" was a dirty word in serious research — the Overton window can flip in 5 years.
- LLMs are Plato's cave — shadows of a world they've never directly seen; spatial intelligence is the next layer.
- Robotics needs the bitter lesson *and* a new data strategy — no internet-scale dataset for the physical world.
- Humanist AI: ethics and human outcomes are the substrate, not a bolt-on.
- Intellectual fearlessness: pick the problem that scares you; ImageNet was dismissed before it became foundational.
