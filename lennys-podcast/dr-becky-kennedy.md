# Dr. Becky Kennedy — Sturdy leadership: the pilot, the repair, and the Most Generous Interpretation

Source: `sources/dr-becky-kennedy.md` (Lenny's Podcast, clinical psychologist, Good Inside)

Dr. Becky's parenting frameworks map cleanly onto leadership. The thesis: behavior and identity are separable, perfect is creepy, and the real leadership skill is sturdiness — the pilot who stays calm when the plane hits turbulence. Her frames (MGI, repair, connect-before-correct, locate someone) are designed for moments of conflict, not moments of ease.

## Good Inside — separate behavior from identity
- Core axiom: a person is always good inside, even when behavior is bad.
- Labels the behavior ("that was a cruel thing to say") without labeling the person ("you are cruel").
- Enables course correction without identity damage; essential for both kids and direct reports.

## Repair > perfection — "perfect is creepy"
- You will rupture: snap, misread, show up wrong. Repair is the actual relationship-building move.
- Repair formula: name what happened, take responsibility without excusing, state what you'll do differently.
- "Perfect is creepy" — people don't trust leaders who never mess up; they trust leaders who repair visibly.

## Sturdy leadership — the pilot metaphor
- Three pilot responses to turbulence: (1) freaks out, (2) pretends nothing's happening, (3) acknowledges + steadies. Only #3 builds trust.
- Sturdy ≠ calm-all-the-time. Sturdy = able to hold your own feelings while also holding someone else's.
- "We're on the same team" is the sturdy leader's opening move in conflict.

## Most Generous Interpretation (MGI)
- Before reacting, default to the most generous reading of the other person's behavior.
- Not naïve — it's tactical: the generous reading is usually closer to true, and it unlocks a better conversation either way.
- In practice: "what would have to be true for this to make sense?" — then start from there.

## Connect before correct
- Feelings overpower skills: a person in emotional dysregulation cannot access the skills they already have.
- Sequence: connect (acknowledge the feeling) → then correct (teach the behavior).
- Inverting the order (correct first) teaches that being upset is unacceptable, which suppresses rather than develops regulation.

## Boundaries — what YOU will do
- Misdefinition: "a boundary is what I want from you." That's a request, not a boundary.
- Real boundary: what *you* will do that requires *nothing* from the other person.
- Example: not "stop yelling at me" but "if the yelling continues I will leave the room."
- Makes the boundary enforceable unilaterally — the other party can't block it.

## Locate someone — have a POV
- To "locate" a person is to have a clear, felt sense of who they are and what they need.
- Leader's job: locate each report so they feel seen, not managed.
- Requires the leader to hold their own POV too — can't locate someone from a mushy, opinionless place.

## Resilience over happiness
- The goal isn't a happy kid (or employee) — it's a resilient one who can tolerate hard feelings.
- Protecting from all distress prevents the development of the exact muscle you need them to have.

## Key Takeaways
- Separate behavior from identity: "that was a cruel thing to say" not "you are cruel."
- Repair is the relationship-building move, not perfection. "Perfect is creepy."
- Sturdy leadership = the pilot who acknowledges turbulence and steadies the plane; "we're on the same team."
- Most Generous Interpretation first — "what would have to be true for this to make sense?"
- Connect before correct; feelings overpower skills.
- A boundary is what YOU will do, not what you want from someone else.
- Aim for resilience, not happiness — protecting from distress prevents the muscle from growing.
