# Matt LeMay — Would You Fund Your Own Team?

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast, Matt LeMay (author, *Product Management in Practice*, *Impact-First Product Teams*)

Matt LeMay's one career-saving question: *If you were CEO, would you fully fund your own team?* Most PMs can't answer quickly — and that hesitation is exactly the risk. His Impact-First framework is an antidote to OKR theater and the "low-impact death spiral."

## The one question
- "If you were the CEO of this company, would you fully fund your own team?"
- Most PMs pause. That pause is the signal.
- If you can't answer *yes* confidently, no one else in the org is better positioned to. You are the closest person to the evidence.

## Low-impact PM death spiral
- Teams take on low-impact work because it's safer — smaller blast radius, less exec scrutiny.
- Analogy: adding rhinestones to the car instead of working on the engine.
- With 10 teams all adding rhinestones, the hood gets too heavy to lift. Now you can't fix the engine even if you want to.
- Outcome: program-management layers grow, dependencies multiply, low-impact begets low-impact — until the next round of layoffs.

## Sweating the middle
- Most OKR / strategy / initiative / bet frameworks are *middle layers* between impact and day-to-day work.
- Teams spend OKR season sweating those middle layers and forget whether any of it adds up to company impact.
- Nobody ever saved a company by scoring 0.7 across five objectives while the business tanked.

## Goals one step from company goals
- From Christina Wodtke's *Radical Focus*: company goal at center, team goals orbiting one layer out.
- Good team goal should be ~one Y statement or one mathematical operator from the company goal.
- MailChimp story: VP set one metric — % of users who successfully send their first email. That single target cut through the platform-domain sprawl.
- FinTech story: "convert single-product users to multi-product users." One multiplier (LTV delta) connected the team goal directly to revenue.

## Impact first at every step
- Not a one-time goal-setting ceremony. Re-anchor on impact at every stage: OKRs, strategy, epics, sprints.
- Growth-team anecdote: full OKR process, 5×5 objectives, then Matt asked "how many new users did we add?" — nobody knew. The team lead restarted with "1M on the whiteboard, our tick is here, every conversation starts here."

## Courage + radical acceptance
- Impact-focused work means accountability for things outside your control (market, competitors, customer behavior).
- The fear of that accountability is rational but misdirected — "all your OKRs were 0.7" won't save your job if the company dies.
- Surprise finding from Matt's research: commercially minded PMs were also the *happiest*. They stopped fighting best-practice battles and went home at 6 pm.

## Embrace your constraints
- Most PMs don't work at Big Tech. They work at banks, insurers, CPG, B2B, regulated industries.
- The constraints (regulation, quarterly targets, enterprise sales cycles) *are* the shape of the work. Not excuses, not impediments — guides.
- B2B is often *easier* than consumer because customers are immediately reachable and systems are traceable.

## PMs as "the whole team thinks like a CEO"
- The mini-CEO framing is often misread: the PM carries all the commercial thinking alone.
- Correct framing: the PM's job is to get *the whole team* — engineers, designers, researchers — reasoning commercially.
- In practice, engineers often reach the impact-level insight first because they see the system.

## Three steps to become an impact-first team
1. Set team goals no more than one step away from company goals.
2. Keep impact first at every step — don't let it cascade into oblivion.
3. (Implied, builds across the book) Be willing to tell the business honestly what's possible and what isn't — proactive downward adjustment beats passive missed targets.

## Key Takeaways
- The CEO question is a 30-second diagnostic for whether your team is safe.
- The low-impact death spiral is the default outcome, not the exception.
- OKR theater is a decoy — the middle layers don't save you.
- Team goals should be one multiplier or one Y-statement from the company goal.
- Treat constraints (regulation, B2B, quarterly targets) as shape, not prison.
- Impact-focused PMs are happier and more employable.
- PM = the person who makes the whole team think commercially, not the person who thinks commercially alone.
