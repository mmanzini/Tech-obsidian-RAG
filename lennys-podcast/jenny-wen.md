# Jenny Wen — Don't trust the design process: designing inside an AI research org

Source: `sources/jenny-wen.md` (Lenny's Podcast, design lead at Anthropic)

Wen's through-line: the classic design process (discover → define → design → deliver, 2–5 year vision decks) is broken in an AI org where the underlying capability changes every few weeks. Her reframe is a stratified model — execution support + 3–6 month vision, not multi-year — with Figma still earning its place as an exploration tool and a specific archetype mix for hiring.

## "Don't trust the design process"
- The textbook double-diamond assumes stable technology and a knowable user need.
- In AI, the capability shifts under you mid-project — a polished flow designed for GPT-4 is wrong when GPT-5 ships.
- Don't throw out design thinking — but don't let the process be the deliverable. Trust the outcome, not the ritual.

## Stratify design into two layers
- **Execution support** — near-term, ship-this-week design work on shipping surfaces.
- **3–6 month vision** — not 2–5 years. The horizon is shorter because the substrate moves faster.
- Skipping the vision layer = reactive design; skipping execution layer = irrelevant design. Both layers are required.

## The 60 → 30% mocking ratio
- Previously: ~60% of design time in mocks (Figma frames).
- Now: ~30% in mocks, the rest in prototyping, prompting, writing PRDs, exploring with the actual AI.
- Mocks still matter — but they're no longer the center of gravity.

## Figma still useful — for exploring 8–10 options
- AI tools are great for one-shot "plausible" output; Figma is still the best place to explore 8–10 parallel variants and compare them.
- The comparison surface matters as much as any individual design.
- Don't let "design in prompts" eat the visual divergence step.

## Research-preview / trust-through-speed
- At Anthropic, research previews ship fast and rough — explicitly labeled as previews.
- Trust is built through shipping velocity, not through polish. Users forgive roughness when the cadence is honest.
- Claude Cowork: 10 days was *just* the final ship sprint, not the whole project — the design exploration long predated it.

## Three hiring archetypes
- **Block-shaped generalists** — can do research, IC design, PM-adjacent thinking, write; no single super-specialty but the whole kit.
- **Deep T-shaped specialists** — one very deep skill (motion, systems, brand) plus enough breadth to plug in.
- **Craft new grads** — fresh grads with obsessive craft sensibility; hire for taste, train the rest.
- A team needs all three. Stacking only one archetype fails predictably.

## IDE shift — from engineers to designers/PMs
- IDEs (Cursor, Claude Code, etc.) are becoming the primary creation surface for designers and PMs, not just engineers.
- Prototyping in code is now faster than prototyping in Figma for many flows.
- Implication: design hiring bar increasingly includes "comfortable in an IDE."

## Manager → IC rotation for empathy
- Anthropic rotates managers back into IC work periodically.
- Not as punishment or demotion — as calibration. A manager who hasn't built in 18 months can't lead designers building today.
- Especially critical in AI where "how the work is done" changes quarterly.

## Key Takeaways
- Don't trust the design process — trust outcomes. The double diamond assumes stable substrate; AI doesn't have one.
- Stratify design into execution support + 3–6 month vision. Not 2–5 years — the horizon is shorter now.
- Mocks dropped from ~60% to ~30% of design time; the rest is prototyping, prompting, writing.
- Figma still matters specifically for exploring 8–10 parallel options — AI tools don't replace divergence.
- Research previews build trust through shipping speed, not polish; Claude Cowork's 10 days was only the ship sprint.
- Hire three archetypes: block-shaped generalists, T-shaped specialists, craft new grads.
- Rotate managers back to IC — can't lead what you haven't recently done.
