# Jeanne Grosser — World-Class GTM in 2026 (Vercel, Stripe, Google)

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast — COO at Vercel; built early sales at Stripe

Jeanne's remit spans marketing, sales, customer success, RevOps, and field engineering. She treats GTM as a product and argues AI is collapsing GTM's 17 specialized roles back into a tighter lifecycle.

## The GTM Engineer

- New role emerging in the last 18–24 months: technical GTM person who builds agents against the sales stack.
- Vercel's first 3 GTM engineers were **sales engineers with CS degrees** (not ML engineers) — GTM domain knowledge is the binding constraint.
- Shadow the highest-performing human in a function, encode their workflow, then turn it into an agent with human-in-the-loop.
- Lead agent built by **one GTM engineer at ~30% of his time, 6 weeks**, replaced 9 of 10 SDRs (9 moved to outbound).
- Cost: **~$1,000/year** to run the agent vs. $1M+ in SDR salary.
- Stripe tried this in 2017 ("Project Roslyn" — mapping the company universe) — it failed without AI; now it works.

## Lost Bot → Deal Bot

- Feed Gong transcripts + Slack + email into an agent to diagnose lost deals.
- Surprising insight: a deal "lost on price" was really lost because **no economic buyer was ever engaged**.
- Moved agent from post-mortem to real-time: pings Slack channels mid-deal with coaching ("You're this far in and haven't talked to an EB").
- Treats go-to-market issues as **bugs**, runs weekly enablement sprints to patch them.

## GTM as Product

- When tech commoditizes, **the buying experience** becomes the differentiator.
- Stripe replaced "discovery calls" with **whiteboarding sessions** — customer leaves with an asset (their own payments architecture drawn), Stripe leaves with competitive intel.
- Add value at every touchpoint even when the customer won't buy — they come back 3-5 years later in a new cycle.
- 80% of enterprise buyers buy to **avoid pain/reduce risk**, not to capture upside. Art-of-the-possible pitches only resonate with other founders.

## Segmentation as a Graph, Not a List

- X-axis at Stripe: company size (SMB/MM/ENT). Y-axis: growth potential (consumption business rewards 200% YoY growers).
- Further cut by **business model** (B2B, B2C, platform, marketplace) — each maps to a different Stripe product line.
- At Vercel: Y-axis is **Crux rank** (Chrome's site traffic data) + **workload type** (e-commerce vs. AI SaaS vs. crypto).
- Max 3 attributes — more than that and segments become unreasonable.
- Segmentation is a **company concern**, not a GTM concern — PMs get her KYC talk in week one.

## The Engineer-Friendly Sales Org Litmus Test

- *"If you put my AE in front of 10 engineers, it should take them 10 minutes to figure out they aren't a PM."*
- Requires product depth deep enough to have credibility with engineering.
- Best GTM orgs are half **revenue-driving, half R&D** — 20 sellers talk to more customers in a week than any UXR team; that signal must flow to the roadmap.

## Hot Takes

- Hire RevOps (analytics) earlier than you think — it's what tells founders what's actually working.
- No $100B company is PLG-only. PLG has a ceiling; sales must be layered in before it caps you.
- Forward-deployed engineering is a rebrand of professional services — and genuinely valuable in the AI era.
- Experimental Run Rate (ERR): the AI version of ARR — customers sign year-long pilots with TBD renewal.

## Key Takeaways

- GTM engineer (not AI engineer) is the new high-leverage hire — profile: sales engineer who can code.
- A $1K/year agent replaced 9 of 10 SDRs at Vercel in 6 weeks.
- 80% of enterprise buying is risk reduction, not upside capture.
- Segment with X-axis = size, Y-axis = a revenue-predicting attribute (growth rate, traffic).
- The litmus test for a great GTM hire: engineers mistake them for a PM.
- Build agents against your own workflows before buying a GTM-agent SaaS.
