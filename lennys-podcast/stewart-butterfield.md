# Stewart Butterfield — Tilt your umbrella: the craft of noticing what everyone else shrugs off

Source: `Raw/stewart-butterfield.md` (Lenny's Podcast, Slack/Flickr co-founder)

Butterfield's recurring move is to treat mundane "of course it works that way" details as actual design surface. His frameworks are about seeing utility curves bend, catching the moments when friction masquerades as inevitability, and killing beloved projects with cold rationality.

## Utility curves (the S-curve of product value)
- Most features follow an S-curve: flat, steep, flat again. The work is knowing which regime you're in.
- "Magic link" email login is the canonical example — 100x improvement over passwords, but only obvious after someone tilts their umbrella to notice the friction everyone else tolerated.
- Craft = a compulsion to notice when the utility curve is about to bend and lean hard into that inflection.

## Tilt your umbrella / "don't make me think"
- Craft is paying attention to the tiny moment a user hesitates, clicks twice, or gets a vague feeling of "huh?" — most teams shrug these off as edge cases.
- The test: would a smart person enjoy using this? Not tolerate — enjoy.
- Opposite failure mode: the "shouty rooster" — a feature that announces itself loudly without actually adding comprehension.

## Hyper-realistic work-like activities vs. known-valuable work
- Orgs drift into elaborate rituals (OKR reviews, status decks, planning offsites) that *look* like work but have uncertain payoff.
- The discipline: ruthlessly separate "known valuable work" (ships value to a customer) from activities that merely simulate it.
- Parkinson's Law compounds this — headcount creates meetings which create coordination which justifies more headcount.

## Owner's delusion + cold-rational pivots
- Founders systematically overvalue their own asset (Glitch before Slack, Game Neverending before Flickr).
- The pivot move requires stepping outside and asking: if I weren't already running this, would I start it today? Usually no.
- Both Flickr and Slack came from killing games Butterfield loved — not from iterating on them.

## "We don't sell saddles here"
- Famous internal memo reframe: Slack wasn't selling chat software, it was selling organizational transformation.
- Marketing/positioning isn't about describing the product — it's about describing the *change* the customer is buying.
- Same logic applies to category creation: the product is the vehicle, not the destination.

## Generosity as cooperative game theory
- In repeated games, generosity compounds — help people without tracking the ledger and the network repays asymmetrically over time.
- "In the long run the measure of our success is value created for customers" — corollary: short-term extraction destroys the repeat game.

## Key Takeaways
- Utility curves are S-shaped; craft is noticing when one is about to bend and throwing weight into that inflection.
- Most teams can't distinguish known-valuable work from hyper-realistic work-like activities — Parkinson's Law fills the gap.
- Pivots require cold rationality: would you start this today if you weren't already in it?
- Positioning is selling the change, not the product ("we don't sell saddles here").
- The magic-link pattern is the test case: 100x friction reduction hiding in plain sight because everyone tolerated passwords.
- Generosity is the dominant long-run strategy in repeated games — don't track the ledger.
