# Brian Halligan — Adults Table, Spiky Teams, Shit Sandwiches

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast, Brian Halligan (HubSpot co-founder, ex-CEO)

Brian Halligan's thesis: at 100 employees the job changes. Below that you're playing at the kids' table — you can compensate for any weakness with hustle. Above it, the game is talent density, spiky 4-out-of-4 hires, and absorbing shit sandwiches without complaining.

## Kids' table vs. adults' table
- Under ~100 employees: kids' table. You can out-work most problems; CEO as player-coach.
- Over ~100: adults' table. The company is now a talent-allocation machine, and your single biggest job is recruiting.
- 50% of CEO time post-100 should be recruiting. If it isn't, you're managing the wrong company.

## Hire slow, fire fast (but actually do it)
- Parker Conrad's NDA board-deck hiring hack: pass the confidential operating data to candidates under NDA. Signals seriousness; filters out people who won't engage.
- Blind reference calls with one killer question: "Would you enthusiastically rehire this person?" Anything short of "yes, immediately" is a no.

## Spiky over smooth
- Hire 4-out-of-4s where you can, 2-out-of-4s with one extreme spike where you can't.
- Never hire 3s across the board. A team of competent generalists loses to a team of spiky specialists.
- The Grateful Dead lesson: the band was spiky and weird — that's *why* it worked. Sand off the edges and you get nothing.

## Avoid McKinsey and big-company hires
- People who succeeded at McKinsey / GE / Oracle optimize for process, legibility, and climbing.
- Those skills are actively toxic in a 50–200 person growth-stage company that needs speed and stomach.
- LOCKS filter for hiring — Lovable, Obsessed, Chip-on-shoulder, Knowledgeable, Student. Screen on these, not on pedigree.

## 2004 Red Sox team-building
- The Red Sox that broke the curse were homegrown core + two or three key free agents.
- Translate: build from within, but be willing to pay up for the 1–2 outside hires that unlock the championship.
- Most startups flip the ratio and lose.

## Halliganisms (CEO operating phrases)
- **"When you eat a shit sandwich, don't nibble."** (Ruth Porat.) Take the whole bite — layoffs, write-downs, bad quarters. Nibbling prolongs the pain and destroys trust.
- **"Never waste a crisis."** The only time you can cut the dead wood and reset expectations cleanly.
- **"Kill a plant with two waterers."** Every initiative needs one DRI. Co-owners means no owner.
- **"Two steps forward, one step back."** The expected rhythm of scaling. Don't panic at the step back.

## EV > TV > MEV
- Three levels of how employees evaluate decisions:
  - **MEV** — My Enterprise Value (personal career, comp)
  - **TV** — Team Value (my squad)
  - **EV** — Enterprise Value (the whole company)
- Good culture trains people to reason EV-first. Most cultures reward MEV-first and then wonder why politics wins.

## Customer-centric → employee-centric shift
- The 2010s playbook: customer obsession wins.
- The 2020s playbook: employee obsession wins, and customer obsession follows. Talent is now the scarcer input.
- Five-tool CEO: product, sales, marketing, recruiting, storytelling. Weak on any of the five and the stock compounds slower.

## Repeat yourself constantly
- You're sick of the message long before the org has heard it.
- Flash-tag system for email: simple tags (e.g., `!urgent`, `!fyi`) so the org learns your signal hierarchy.
- Snowmobile-accident motto: "life's short." Ship fast, say the hard thing, don't save it for the next offsite.

## Key Takeaways
- 100 employees is the inflection; above it, recruiting is half your calendar.
- Spiky 4-of-4 hires and 2-of-4 specialists beat uniform 3-of-4s every time.
- Avoid big-company refugees in growth-stage orgs.
- Eat the whole shit sandwich, never waste a crisis, one DRI per plant.
- EV > TV > MEV is how you describe healthy culture in one line.
- Employee-centric is the new customer-centric.
- A CEO is a five-tool athlete and a broken record — both at once.
