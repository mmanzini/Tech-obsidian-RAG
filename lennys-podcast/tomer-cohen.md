# Tomer Cohen — AI-First PMs Control the Pedals

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast, Tomer Cohen (CPO, LinkedIn)

Tomer's theory of the AI-era PM: AI is not the driver, the PM is — but they're driving a car with very different pedals. He ran a 2M-member carve-out to turn around the LinkedIn feed, forces his org through an "AI Academy," and tells his teams "I might be wrong, but I'm not confused." AI is the ultimate matchmaker; the PM's job is to keep steering toward the mountain peak while diverging broadly before converging.

## "Might be wrong but not confused"
- Tomer's signature line and internal operating phrase.
- Distinguishes *conviction with humility* from *hedging*: he's willing to be wrong about the specific path, but not fuzzy about the destination.
- Used as a filter for PM work: if you can't state the direction clearly enough to be wrong about it, you're not ready to lead.

## 2M-member carve-out to turn around Feed
- LinkedIn's feed was stuck. Tomer's fix: carve out a 2M-member segment and give a dedicated team end-to-end control over that segment's feed experience.
- Small cohort, fast iteration, no cross-product dependencies — classic skunkworks played inside a platform.
- Once the carve-out proved new patterns at scale, they rolled back out to 100%.
- Connects to [[ai-organization/_index|AI & Organisation Design]] on segment-scoped teams.

## AI-first mindset — PMs control the pedals
- The metaphor: AI is the car, the PM is the driver. The pedals (gas, brake, steering) are the same job they've always been — just responding to different machinery underneath.
- AI-first PMs don't ask "how do I add AI to this product?"; they ask "given AI in the stack, what's the right product?"
- The failure mode is PMs who let the model dictate the roadmap; the model is horsepower, not direction.

## AI as the ultimate matchmaker
- LinkedIn is a matching business (jobs, people, content, skills). AI makes matching *the* core capability, not a feature.
- Tomer's framing: stop thinking of AI as a feature layer — treat it as the substrate the whole product rides on.
- Implication: the PM's job is to design the matching experience, not prompt-engineer individual features.

## Mountain-peak visualization
- Tomer asks his teams to *visualize the mountain peak* — the 3-year endgame of the product — before any quarterly planning.
- The peak is stable; the trail to it is replanned constantly.
- Without a shared peak, teams drift into incrementalism and AI experimentation becomes noise.

## Diverge-then-converge AI bets
- Deliberate portfolio pattern: diverge first (many small AI bets across surfaces), then converge (double down on the 2–3 that move metrics).
- Resists the temptation to converge too early when everyone wants to be "doing AI."
- Frames failure of early bets as *information*, not waste.

## AI Academy and the talent flywheel
- LinkedIn runs an internal "AI Academy" — mandatory training for PMs on AI-native product work, not tools training.
- Goal: every PM should be able to scope, brief, and evaluate AI-backed features without needing the ML team to translate.
- The learning curve is cultural more than technical.

## Key Takeaways
- "I might be wrong but I'm not confused" — conviction on direction, humility on path.
- Carve out a small member segment to turn a stuck product around, then roll back up.
- AI is the engine; the PM is still the driver controlling the pedals.
- AI is not a feature — it's the matching substrate the product rides on.
- Visualize the 3-year mountain peak before the next quarter.
- Diverge broadly before converging on the 2–3 AI bets that actually move metrics.
