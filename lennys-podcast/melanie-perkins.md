# Melanie Perkins — Column B thinking: get to the future by refusing Column A trade-offs

Source: `Raw/melanie-perkins.md` (Lenny's Podcast, Canva co-founder/CEO)

Perkins' operating system is built on reframing every "either/or" into "both/and" and pursuing Crazy Big Goals by grinding through absurd amounts of rejection. The distinctive moves are her Column B thinking, the two-step plan, the rubber-ducky game board, and the mission-pillar discipline that forces product to hit every ingredient, language and device.

## Column B thinking
- Column A = the trade-off the industry has accepted (powerful OR easy, pro OR consumer).
- Column B = the version where you refuse the trade-off and build the bridge.
- Canva was Column B for design: the power of Photoshop *and* the accessibility of PowerPoint, not either/or.
- The question to ask every PM debate: what would Column B look like, even if we don't know how to build it yet?

## Crazy Big Goals + chaos-to-clarity
- Goals should be uncomfortably large — "empower the world to design" is the Canva version.
- The founder's job in early stage is specifically **chaos to clarity**: transform fuzzy ambition into concrete next action, over and over.
- Pattern: 100+ investor rejections before Canva's seed round; each rejection was an iteration on the pitch, not a verdict.

## The two-step plan
- Step 1: build one of the most valuable companies in the world.
- Step 2: use it to do the most good.
- Explicitly two steps, not one — trying to collapse them leads to either a charity that can't scale or a company with no conviction about impact.
- Profits and mission are not in tension if you sequence them honestly.

## Mission pillars — every ingredient, every language, every device
- Canva's product bar is expressed as pillars that cannot be dropped: every design ingredient (image/font/template), every language, every device.
- Forces the org to reject "we'll ship English first and localize later" or "web-first, mobile-later" — those are Column A thinking.
- Two-year front-end rewrite was accepted because pillars made it non-negotiable.

## Rubber-ducky game board
- Visual planning artifact: the strategy is a physical game board with rubber ducks, not a slide deck.
- Forces tangibility — if you can't place a duck on the board, the plan isn't real.
- Surfaces gaps and sequencing in a way text-based roadmaps hide.

## Closing the loop at scale
- Canva receives ~1M community requests/year; ~200 loops closed (shipped, communicated back to requester).
- The ratio doesn't matter as much as the fact that the loop closes at all — it's a trust primitive.
- Scales a feeling of "they listen" that would be impossible to fake via broadcasts.

## AI walk
- Perkins blocks time for a daily "AI walk" — just her, headphones, and the latest models, talking through strategy out loud.
- Treats AI as thinking partner, not task executor — the walk makes it a ritual, not a tool she forgets to use at her desk.

## Key Takeaways
- Column B thinking: refuse industry trade-offs; build the "both/and" even when the path is unclear.
- Crazy Big Goals require a founder skill of chaos-to-clarity — 100 rejections iterate the pitch, not verdicts.
- Two-step plan: build the most valuable company first, then do the most good — don't collapse the steps.
- Mission pillars (every ingredient/language/device) make "we'll ship a subset" structurally impossible.
- Rubber-ducky game board makes strategy tangible — if a duck can't go on it, the plan isn't real.
- Close the loop on user requests even at 1M-scale; the ratio is less important than the fact that some requests do come back.
