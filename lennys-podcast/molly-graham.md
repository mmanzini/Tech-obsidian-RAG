# Molly Graham — Give Away Your Legos (and Five Other Scaling Tools)

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast — Scaling operator (ex-Facebook, ex-Quip, ex-Chan Zuckerberg)

Molly's operating frame: scaling isn't about adding people — it's about people repeatedly handing off the thing they love. Her toolkit externalizes the emotional cost of that handoff so it doesn't stall the org.

## Give Away Your Legos

- Scaling = building a house, passing it to someone else, starting the neighborhood.
- The handoff feels like loss because the old thing was yours.
- The emotional arc: pride → grief → relief → "I can build bigger now."
- Leaders who can't give away Legos become the bottleneck their own company outgrows.

## Bob the Monster — Externalize the Feeling

- Name the scary/sad/anxious feeling ("Bob") so it stops running the conversation.
- Works 1:1 and in groups: "OK, Bob is in the room, what is Bob saying?"
- Makes it socially safe to admit the handoff hurts without derailing work.
- Reduces decision paralysis caused by unspoken emotions.

## The Two-Week Rule

- Give yourself (and your team) **two weeks** to feel bad after any org change.
- After two weeks: the new structure is the structure; stop relitigating it.
- Prevents the long tail of micro-sabotage from people who never accepted the change.
- Paired with explicit leader permission: "it's OK to be sad, and it's also two weeks."

## J-Curve vs Stairs — The Chamath Facebook Phone Story

- Scaling is a **J-curve**, not a staircase — productivity dips before it climbs.
- Chamath's Facebook team tried the phone app: a year of pain, then explosive growth.
- Most orgs abandon at the bottom of the J because they expected stairs.
- Leadership job: narrate the J out loud so the team knows the dip is expected, not failure.

## The Waterline Model — Snorkel Before Scuba

- Four layers of team problems, shallowest to deepest:
  1. **Structural** (roles, ownership, reporting lines)
  2. **Dynamics** (how the team interacts)
  3. **Interpersonal** (specific relationships)
  4. **Intrapersonal** (individual's inner state)
- **80% of "team problems" are actually structural** — unclear roles and goals.
- Always snorkel the shallow layer first. Going straight to interpersonal coaching when the org chart is broken is malpractice.

## Six Rules for Goals That Work

1. **≤3 goals per team** — more than three means none.
2. **One wins** — rank explicitly; tied priorities paralyze.
3. **Explain-to-a-5-year-old** test — if you can't, rewrite it.
4. **Strategy should hurt** — if saying yes to this doesn't cost something obvious, it isn't strategy.
5. **One owner per goal** — accountability cannot be shared.
6. **Accountability process** — weekly or biweekly review with decisions made visibly.

## "All Advice Is Just Someone Telling You What They Did"

- Credited to Ami Vora.
- Advice is an artifact of the advisor's context — it is not a prescription.
- Listen, understand the context, then decide if yours matches.
- Applies strongly to Molly's own frameworks: use what maps, discard what doesn't.

## Key Takeaways

- Scaling = giving away your Legos; the emotional cost is real and must be named.
- Use "Bob the monster" to externalize fear; give everyone a 2-week grief window.
- Expect the J-curve; narrate the dip so the team doesn't interpret it as failure.
- Waterline Model: 80% of team problems are structural — fix roles before coaching.
- ≤3 goals, one owner each, one wins; strategy should hurt.
- All advice is contextual — the advisor's story, not your playbook.
