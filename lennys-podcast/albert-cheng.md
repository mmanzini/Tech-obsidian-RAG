# Albert Cheng — Explore/Exploit, Reverse Trials, and Resurrected Users

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast — Growth lead (ex-Duolingo, Grammarly)

Albert ran growth at Duolingo during the "Green Machine" era and led strategic experiments at Grammarly. His frame: most growth teams collapse into exploit mode; durable growth requires a mechanical oscillation between exploring and exploiting, measured in experiments shipped per year.

## Explore / Exploit as an Oscillation

- Growth teams default to exploit: tune existing funnels, win small lifts, starve of new channels.
- Mandate: every quarter has a budgeted **explore allocation** (25-40% of experiment slots).
- Targets: new loops, new surfaces, new user cohorts — accept high failure rate.
- Duolingo goal under Albert: **1,000 experiments/year** with explicit explore/exploit quotas.

## Chess.com Game Review — The 80% Insight

- 80% of Chess.com users opened **game review after wins**, not losses.
- Conventional wisdom says people learn from mistakes; the data says they celebrate wins.
- The product was redesigned to be post-win-first, which moved retention.
- Generalization: your best insight often contradicts the learning narrative you've been told.

## Cross-Team Insight Propagation

- Build a **text-to-SQL Slack bot** so every PM can query data without waiting on analysts.
- Insights found by one team must move across the org in days, not quarters.
- Duolingo ran weekly cross-functional forums where every team presented one surprising metric.
- Culture signal: celebrating insights is as ritualized as celebrating shipped features.

## Grammarly Reverse Free Trial — Giving Away the Paid

- Standard consumer SaaS D1 retention baseline: **30-40%**.
- Grammarly experiment: free users received **paid suggestions temporarily**, then lost them.
- Upgrade conversion **nearly doubled** — loss aversion outperformed positive framing.
- Generalisation: showing what's missing beats advertising what's included.

## Resurrected Users Need Their Own Product

- Users who return after 60+ days of inactivity are **not new users** — they have outdated context.
- Onboarding them with the "new user" flow fails; onboarding them with nothing also fails.
- Build a **resurrected experience**: acknowledges time gone, re-demonstrates value, skips known content.
- Often the single highest-ROI growth surface nobody owns.

## Duolingo's "Green Machine" Clock-Speed Culture

- Growth cadence measured in experiments per team per year, surfaced on internal leaderboards.
- No review debates — you ship, you measure, you move.
- Ops investment: in-house experimentation platform, data infra, self-serve insight tooling.
- The culture is the moat — new entrants can't rebuild five years of tooling overnight.

## Consumer Subscription Benchmarks

- D1 retention <30% → serious activation problem.
- D1 30-40% → median; D1 >45% → elite.
- Paid-to-free ratio and reactivation curve are the next diagnostic layers after D1.

## Key Takeaways

- Budget explicit explore quotas so exploit doesn't eat the pipeline.
- Measure growth output in experiments-per-year, not initiatives.
- Your counter-narrative insight (80% review wins) is usually the highest-leverage finding.
- Reverse free trials beat positive framing via loss aversion.
- Resurrected users need their own product surface — not new-user onboarding.
- D1 retention 30-40% is the consumer-sub baseline; <30% is a red flag.
