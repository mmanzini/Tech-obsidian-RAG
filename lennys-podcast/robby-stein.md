# Robby Stein — Search Is Expansionary, Not Zero-Sum

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast, Robby Stein (VP of Product, Google Search)

Robby's counter-narrative to the "AI will kill Google" story: AI makes search *expansionary* — people ask more questions, not fewer. Google's three AI pillars (AI Overviews, AI Mode, Lens) already run dozens of background "query fan-out" searches per user question. Lens is up 70% YoY. The operating principle is "relentless improvement" — his wife's word for him is *dissatisfied* — and his product-launch philosophy is shaped by Tony Fadell's fruit-sticker TED talk.

## Three AI pillars
- **AI Overviews** — the AI-generated summary at the top of results; now the default experience for most queries.
- **AI Mode** — a conversational search surface where you can refine, compare, and follow up.
- **Lens** — visual/camera search, up 70% YoY; the fastest-growing input modality.
- Strategy: meet the user wherever intent is formed — text, voice, camera, conversation.

## Query fan-out
- A single user question now triggers *dozens* of background searches Google runs in parallel to assemble an answer.
- The user sees one answer; the system searched a graph of subqueries behind it.
- This is why "AI cannibalizes search" is a category error — under the hood, AI *multiplies* the number of searches per user intent.
- Connects to [[agent-architecture/_index|Agent Architecture]] subagent patterns.

## AI is expansionary, not cannibalizing
- Robby's data: users with access to AI Overviews and AI Mode ask *more* questions, not fewer.
- Curiosity is elastic — when answering gets easier, people ask more of the things they used to not bother asking.
- Implication: search volume grows alongside AI answers; the business risk is share of intent, not volume of queries.
- Parallel to [[ai-product-development/_index|AI Product Development]]'s Jevons-style findings.

## Relentless improvement — "dissatisfied"
- His wife describes him in one word: *dissatisfied*.
- The operating principle: every surface, every metric, every week. Never-done.
- Google's edge in search has always been 1–2% compounding improvements shipped relentlessly across decades.
- The cultural risk in any category leader is comfort — Robby treats it as an active enemy.

## Tony Fadell's fruit-sticker TED talk
- Influential reference: Fadell's talk on how we stop noticing daily friction (the little stickers on supermarket fruit).
- Robby's PM rule: maintain *fresh eyes* on every surface you ship, because users notice every bit of friction you've stopped seeing.
- Practical habit: walk through your product as a new user weekly; every time something annoys you, file a bug.

## Instagram Stories rationale (format adoption, not theft)
- Robby (who previously led Instagram Stories) reframes the "copied Snapchat" critique: formats migrate because they're the right shape for a behavior.
- Stories-as-format was adopted by every major platform because short, ephemeral, vertical video fit how people wanted to share — not because anyone stole it.
- The lesson for PMs: fight about *formats*, not *features*. The right format is a platform bet; features are tactics.

## J-curve retention
- Stories launched flat, then hit a J-curve inflection as users learned the format.
- Robby's pattern: new formats always look like failures for 3–6 months; the J-curve arrives once critical mass of creators makes the feed worth opening.
- Managerial implication: don't kill format bets on week-2 retention — instrument for the J, not the line.

## Key Takeaways
- AI makes search *expansionary* — users ask more questions when answers get easier.
- Query fan-out: one user question = dozens of background searches assembled into one answer.
- Three pillars — AI Overviews, AI Mode, Lens (+70% YoY) — meet intent wherever it forms.
- "Dissatisfied" as an operating principle; comfort is the category leader's real enemy.
- Fadell's fruit stickers — keep fresh eyes on every surface; file the friction you've stopped seeing.
- Fight over *formats*, not features; expect a J-curve on any new format.
